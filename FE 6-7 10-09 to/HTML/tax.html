India's Economy: GDP and Tax System

Introduction


India is one of the fastest-growing major economies in the world. It has a rich and diverse economic structure.
The Gross Domestic Product (GDP) reflects the total value of goods and services produced, while the Tax System determines how revenue is collected to fund development.
Together, these elements shape India’sfinancial stability and future growth potential.

Gross Domestic Product (GDP)

- Agriculture: ~17% of GDP. Agriculture remains the backbone of rural India, employing over 40% ofthe workforce.
It includes farming, forestry, and fisheries. While its share in GDP has declined over decades, it still plays a vital role in ensuring food security and raw material supply to industries.
- Industry: ~27% of GDP. This sector covers manufacturing, mining, electricity, and construction. India has emerged as a global hub for steel, automobiles, textiles, and pharmaceuticals.
Industrial growth is also linked to infrastructure projects and the "Make in India" initiative.
- Services: ~54% of GDP. The largest contributor to GDP, this includes IT services, finance, healthcare, education, tourism, and transport. The IT-BPM industry alone accounts for billions in exports and haspositioned India as the “Back Office of the World.”

Recent Growth Trends:

  1. 2023-24: GDP growth at 6.5%, reflecting resilience despite global uncertainties
  2. Q1 2025-26: Growth accelerated to 7.8%, largely driven by services and higher consumer demand.
  3. Agriculture: Steady growth at around 3.7% due to monsoon recovery and government support schemes.

Taxation in India


The taxation system in India provides the government with revenue to fund public expenditure, infrastructure, and social programs

Direct Taxes: Paid directly by individuals and companies to the government.
  • Income Tax: Levied on individuals’ earnings based on slabs.
  • Corporate Tax: Paid by companies on profits, contributing significantly to revenue
Indirect Taxes: Collected indirectly through the sale of goods and services
  • Goods and Services Tax (GST): Introduced in 2017, it replaced multiple indirect taxes like VAT , excise, and service tax.
  • Excise Duty: Applied to goods manufactured within the country (now mostly subsumed under GST).

Key Insights
  1. Services remain the main driver of GDP growth, contributing the most to exports and employment in urban areas.
  2. Agriculture, though contributing less to GDP, is vital for rural livelihoods and food security.
